# Advanced Team Dynamics & Leadership - The Team Whisperer

This skill manages human resources, synchronization points, psychological safety, and energy optimization to prevent team burnout and maximize execution speed.

---

## 👥 PART 1: THE NEUROSCIENCE OF TEAM COLLABORATION

### 1. The 4 Stages of Hackathon Team Formation

```
STAGE 1: Forming (Hour 0 - 2)
├─ Team aligns on values, ideas, and tech stacks.
├─ Risk: Ambiguous roles or unspoken expectations.
└─ STRATEGY: Establish a "roles matrix" instantly. Write down who owns frontend, backend, design, and pitch.

STAGE 2: Storming (Hour 2 - 6)
├─ Ideation debates, initial system design disagreements, or setup friction.
├─ Risk: Scope creep or early ego battles.
└─ STRATEGY: Enforce a strict "majority vote or mentor tiebreaker" system. Make decisions in under 15 minutes.

STAGE 3: Norming (Hour 6 - 12)
├─ Code contracts are signed, databases are seeded, APIs are mock-tested.
├─ Risk: Silent blocking (one dev waiting on another).
└─ STRATEGY: Define mock JSON responses immediately so frontend and backend work in parallel.

STAGE 4: Performing (Hour 12 - 24)
├─ Code merges, hardening, and final demo rehearsals.
├─ Risk: Team fatigue, low energy, and drop in communication.
└─ STRATEGY: Schedule compulsory 15-minute screen-free breaks and food rotations. Maintain morale.
```

### 2. Psychological Safety: The Core of High-Performance Teams
High-performing hackathon teams do not have an authoritarian structure; they operate on a high trust scale:
*   **Encourage Vulnerability**: If a team member feels stuck on a bug for more than 20 minutes, they must declare it without fear of judgment.
*   **Blameless Post-Mortems**: If a git merge conflict breaks the staging build, the team focuses on fixing the issue immediately rather than searching for who committed the code.

Optimally allocate skills across the classic hackathon roles:

## ⚡ PART 3: THE 5-MINUTE CONFLICT RESOLUTION PROTOCOL

When disagreements on features, scope, or technology arise under time pressure:
1.  **Stop and Frame (60s)**: Give both sides 30 seconds to state their case strictly in terms of *impact on the MVP* (e.g., *"Doing X will add 3 hours of dev time"* vs *"Doing Y will improve our visual score"*).
2.  **Verify Constraints (60s)**: Map the conflict against our remaining time budget and technical feasibility.
3.  **Vote or Delegate (60s)**: Take a quick majority vote. In the event of a tie, the Tech Lead has final authority on engineering disputes; the Presenter has final authority on product and pitch disputes.
4.  **Commit and Execute (120s)**: Once a decision is made, the entire team commits 100% to the chosen path with no retroactive complaints.
